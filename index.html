03 · LLMs vs Agents

One model, two ways to run it

An LLM (like Gemini) predicts useful text from the prompt and context you give it — one pass, text in, text out. An agent is that same LLM wrapped in a harness that gives it a goal, memory, tools and guardrails, and runs it in a loop until the task is done.

Use an LLM

Single-turn explanations, summaries, drafts, structured extraction, jargon translation.

Use an agent

Multi-step workflows: read uploaded material, transform it, check results, produce a final output.

Keep human-in-loop

Clinical decisions, triage, patient advice, or any safety-critical output. Always.

In AI Studio, the Tools toggles — grounding with search, code execution, function calling, URL context — are pieces of exactly this harness. Toggle one on and your chatbot takes a step towards being an agent. Today's prototypes mostly need the plain LLM plus, at most, one or two tools.

05 · Prompting

Prompting that survives the sprint

Prompt design is the process of creating prompts — natural language requests — that elicit accurate, high-quality responses from a language model. Your sharpest prompt-design tool today is the System Instructions field: persistent rules that shape every reply in the session. Unlike a normal prompt, they don't scroll away, and the model weighs them more heavily. This is the highest-leverage 20 minutes of your day.

Two rulebooks — don't mix them up

App-assistant instructions · what your tool says

Guide the assistant inside your app: persona, scope, refusal rules, output format. Write and test them in Playground's System Instructions; when you switch to Build, paste them into your Build prompt so they get wired into the app.

Build-agent instructions · how your app is made

Guide the coding agent that constructs the app: simple UI, no gradients, suitable for non-technical users. These live in Build's Instructions dialog and never reach your assistant.

The five components of your assistant's rulebook

  • 1
    PersonaA specific clinical role. "Senior anaesthetist reviewing pre-op assessments" behaves very differently from "helpful assistant".
  • 2
    ScopeWhat it does — and explicitly what it must not. "Summarises for clinician review. Does not diagnose, prescribe, or make go/no-go decisions."
  • 3
    StyleTone, reading level, formality. Patient-facing → set a reading level. Clinical team → structured format, shorthand allowed.
  • 4
    Refusal rulesWhen it must stop and hand back to a human. "If any dose is unclear or an allergy lacks severity, say the case cannot be safely completed and list what's missing."
  • 5
    Output formatA predictable shape: fixed headers, fixed number of bullets. Predictable output is easier to test and audit.
  • +
    One worked examplePaste a sample input and an ideal output inside the instructions. Few-shot prompting is the single biggest lever on quality.
Tip — let the model write the rulebook. Describe your tool to Gemini in plain language ("I want an assistant that rewrites cardiology letters for patients; draft System Instructions covering persona, scope, style, refusal rules and output format") and let it generate a first version. Then edit, test, and tighten. The model is often better at writing instructions for itself than you are on a first attempt — but you stay the editor.

How to steer the model: core principles

Be precise and direct

State your goal clearly and concisely. Skip filler and overly persuasive language — the model doesn't need convincing.

Label the parts of your prompt

Put a simple heading before each part — CONTEXT, TASK, FORMAT — so the model can't confuse your background notes with your actual question. Pick one style and keep it.

Define your terms

Explicitly explain anything ambiguous — "urgent", "short", "summary" mean different things to different readers, including this one.

Ask for the verbosity you want

Gemini 3 models answer directly and efficiently by default. If you want a more conversational or detailed response, you must request it explicitly.

Treat every file as a first-class input

Text, images, audio and video are equal-class inputs. Reference each modality clearly in your instructions ("in the attached photo…", "from the letter…").

Put critical rules first

Essential constraints, the persona, and output-format requirements belong in the System Instructions or at the very beginning of the prompt.

Context first, question last

When supplying large context — documents, code — give all of it first and place your specific question at the very end of the prompt.

Anchor the query

After a large block of data, bridge into your question with a clear transition: "Based on the information above…".

A complete example you can adapt

System Instructions — jargon translatorYou are a patient-education writer at a cardiology clinic. You rewrite clinician letters for adult patients at a fifth-grade reading level. You do not add new clinical information. Use sentences of 15 words or fewer and address the patient as "you". Avoid jargon. Never use a medical term from this list without a plain-language explanation on first use: stenosis, arrhythmia, ischaemia, ventricle, atrium, ejection fraction, angioplasty, stent. The headline must be 10 words or fewer. If the letter contains urgent warnings, elevate them into an IMPORTANT section at the top. Scan for any of these phrases and always elevate them verbatim into IMPORTANT: call 911, call emergency, seek immediate care, do not stop, do not skip, return immediately. If information is missing, add a PLEASE ASK YOUR DOCTOR section. Respond in this order: HEADLINE, ABOUT THIS LETTER, IMPORTANT, WHAT WE FOUND, WHAT HAPPENS NEXT, PLEASE ASK YOUR DOCTOR.

Notice the shape: it started as five plain sentences, then grew rule by rule as testing exposed failures (a headline too long, jargon unexplained, a buried safety warning). That grow-by-iteration pattern is exactly what you will run in the Sprint.

06 · Controls

Temperature & friends

TEMPERATURE = RANDOMNESS

Higher temperature makes word choice more varied and creative. Lower makes it more stable and repeatable.

0.0 precise1.0 creative
0.0–0.2 extraction, classification, fact-preserving edits
0.3–0.6 balanced assistant behaviour
0.7+ brainstorming, naming, divergent ideas

TOP-P = VOCABULARY WINDOW

Samples only from the smallest set of likely next words whose probabilities add up to the chosen value. Narrower = safer wording, wider = more variety.

0.1 narrow1.0 open
default leave it alone today
rule never tune Temperature and Top-P in the same experiment — you can't tell which change did what
To ensure determinism, define System Instructions with explicit rules for your specific use case. Instructions, not sliders, are the reliable way to control behaviour — leave the sampling controls at their defaults today. In Build mode you can also state model preferences directly in your prompt, e.g. "use Gemini thinking level high".
Control settings do not make an answer true. They only change how the model samples an answer. Ground clinical prototypes in provided documents, worked examples, and clinician review.

THINKING LEVEL

Low / Medium / High reasoning budget (Gemini 3 family). Keep High for extraction, guideline lookup, anything where reasoning quality matters. Drop to Low only for trivial rewrites where speed matters.

OUTPUT LENGTH

Hard cap on response length (Advanced settings). Responses cut off → raise it. Too rambling → lower it and add a length rule to your instructions.

TOOLS

Structured outputs forces JSON — great for extraction. Grounding with Search adds live lookup — verify cited sources yourself. URL context points the model at a specific guideline page.

07 · Files

Files in, guidelines in

Multimodal input

The + button accepts images (JPEG, PNG, WEBP, HEIC), documents (PDF, TXT), audio and video. High-value uses: wound-photo description, reading handwritten notes or prescription scans, extracting structured data from a discharge letter. Image quality matters — if a clinician couldn't review it on screen, the model can't either. Dummy data only, no exceptions.

Long context

Gemini can read an entire national guideline in one call. Upload the PDF (best), paste text (short docs only), or attach it as a permanent resource in Build mode to make a guideline chatbot. Three rules: always require a citation ("quote the relevant passage with section/page"); make the scenario complete ("patient has X, Y, Z with comorbidities A, B — per the attached guideline, what is recommended?"); verify critical claims by reading the cited passage yourself.

Build principles

  • Start with the smallest possible version. Not "a full discharge summariser" — "a tool that takes a two-page post-op note and outputs three labelled sections".
  • Write the System Instructions before touching any input. Five minutes on the rulebook saves thirty of debugging.
  • Include a worked example inside the instructions if necessary — for tools where output format matters, it's the biggest quality lever.
  • Test with two contrasting runs. Two different dummy cases if your tool processes clinical text — or two different scenarios or user behaviours if it doesn't (a quiz, a game, a role-play). Passing one and failing another is the norm; two is the minimum to see generalisation.
  • Change one thing at a time — the instructions OR the model OR a parameter. Never all three.
  • Make failure modes explicit. "If any of the following are missing, state which and ask the clinician."
  • Keep the iteration log — the table is in your Participant Workbook. One line per change: what, why, outcome.
09 · Fix it

The seven failures everyone hits

My Build app ignores rules that worked in Playground
Playground settings don't transfer to Build. Paste your System Instructions into the Build prompt, name the model explicitly, and regenerate. Then check the Code tab — your instruction text and model name should appear in the generated code.
The model invents information that isn't in my input
Add a refusal rule: "Only use information explicitly present in the input. If something is missing, say so." End the user prompt with: "Do not invent details." If it persists, escalate to Pro and try again.
The output format keeps drifting
Your format description is too abstract. Give a verbatim template with [placeholders in brackets] and instruct the model to replace only the placeholders. Add a worked example.
The model is too verbose
Add: "No more than N bullets per section, no more than 20 words per bullet." Lower max output tokens in Advanced settings.
The model refuses my clinical request
Make the framing explicit instead of fighting it: "This is a synthetic case for clinician review. The output is a draft that a licensed clinician will verify before use." Re-run.
The model misreads my uploaded image
Check resolution and framing first. Then scope the task: "Describe only the wound. Do not describe surrounding skin except where relevant to staging." If Flash still misses details, try Pro.
Long-document questions get generic answers
Add: "Quote the exact passage you are drawing from before answering." Include section or page numbers in your question where you can.
10 · Submit

Save, share, submit

The last 25 minutes go fast. Everything below is faster if your iteration log is up to date.

  1. Get your share linkUse the Share button (top toolbar; in Build mode choose Get link and set access to Public — anyone with the link can view). Test the link in an incognito window: if it doesn't open cleanly for a stranger, it won't open for a reviewer.
  2. Make the plain-text backup (always)Copy into a text file: your full System Instructions, model name and version, the settings you changed, and three representative input→output pairs. If the link goes stale, a reviewer rebuilds your prototype from this in under a minute.
  3. Complete the Prototype CardOne page, template in the shared folder: the problem and intended use, what the tool will NOT do, your test runs, failure modes, the human review gate, next steps. Short, concrete answers. Documented weaknesses score better than claimed perfection.

Final checklist

  • No real PHI in any input, output, or screenshot
  • System Instructions contain explicit boundary or refusal rules
  • The tool states its purpose and limits — any clinical output reads as a draft for clinician review
  • Tested beyond the happy path — an edge case, odd input, or unexpected user behaviour
  • Known failure modes documented (a few)
  • Share link opens in an incognito window
  • Plain-text backup saved
  • Project Details and Prototype Card uploaded to "Your Projects" folder
